The Four Seasons

Explore the Seasons

About

We experience four distinct seasons. These are winter, spring, summer, and fall (or autumn).

Winter: In the United States, winter tends to see people wrap up in thick coats, scarves, hats, and gloves. The coldest months in the Northern Hemisphere (where the United States is situated) tend to be December and January. Temperatures can dip below 0 degrees Celsius and frost can occur. As you get closer to the equator, temperatures rise and the climate becomes less varied.

Spring: In spring, the bare tree branches come to life again with greenery. Humans can transition from their thicker winter coats to something a little lighter. However, rainfall can still be pronounced throughout spring, and while milder, spring days can still carry quite the chill. Taking an umbrella with you on outings is a must, especially if you live in a region where the weather can be unpredictable.

Summer: Summer is the warmest season of the year. With our increased understanding of global warming and climate change, scientists are noticing that our summers are getting hotter. Temperatures can hit record highs, and it's not unusual to experience days, if not weeks, of temperatures over 30 degrees Celsius. If temperatures become too high, they can cause issues for animals, plants, and humans.

Fall (Autumn): As temperatures cool once more, the trees can begin to change color, leaves can be shed, and animals begin to store their food for the winter. Some animals, such as birds, migrate to warmer climates, so they don't have to adapt to cooler climates.
